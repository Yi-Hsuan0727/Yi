Introduction

Overview

As a UI/UX Designer, I collaborated with a multidisciplinary team to develop the interface ecosystem for AgriDrone, a multi-role agricultural workstation UAV system. Unlike specialized drones limited to a single purpose, AgriDrone is a flexible platform that adapts to various farming needs through interchangeable payloads, advanced imaging, and smart sensors.

Because the system requires three operators working in tandem, the UI was divided among our design team. While my teammates designed the Pilot and Specialist interfaces, I took full ownership of designing the Technician Interface, creating a workspace optimized for payload management, real-time troubleshooting, and system maintenance.

01 — Problem Statement

Complexity is the enemy of usability

American farmers manage an average of 466 acres. Monitoring crops and tracking livestock typically involves manual visual inspections, soil sampling, and headcounts done on foot or by truck, which is both slow and expensive.

466 acres

Average farm size — manual monitoring scales poorly across large plots.

$15,000+

Typical cost per unitask drone system — most farmers cannot justify purchasing three separate systems for mapping, spraying, and thermal imaging.

AgriDrone solves this by combining these capabilities into one flexible platform. However, managing a multi-tool drone inherently creates complexity. For a Field Technician working 12-hour shifts, complexity is the enemy of usability. I needed to design a display that made managing attachments, diagnostics, and repairs intuitive and manageable without causing cognitive overload.

02 — Project Goals

Guiding objectives for the Technician interface

Role-Specific Focus

Design a tailored, uncluttered tablet display exclusively for the Technician / Payload Operator, who directs cameras, spraying systems, cargo modules, and technical repairs.

Apply core human factors principles so the technician can quickly interpret system health, respond to critical alerts, and troubleshoot without split attention.

Seamless Ecosystem

Ensure the Technician interface works in perfect harmony with the Pilot and Specialist interfaces designed by my team.

1. Research & Concept Development

We began by analyzing the workflow of agricultural operations and writing a comprehensive white paper outlining our multi-role concept. We determined the drone would act as a “flying diagnostic lab.” My specific challenge was figuring out how a technician could monitor interchangeable parts—like a sprayer, seeder, or ground-penetrating radar—on a single screen without confusion.

2. Information Architecture & Wireframing

I structured the Technician Interface to ensure the operator only saw information critical to their specific responsibilities. I broke the workspace down into three core dashboards:

Overview Dashboard

For at-a-glance health monitoring of the UAV and its current attachments.

Mission Dashboard

For tracking the active task (e.g., spraying zones, usage trends).

Statistics Dashboard

For reviewing post-flight data, maintenance logs, and historical performance.

3. Iteration & Applying Human Factors

Translating dense diagnostic data into a user-friendly UI required rigorous application of cognitive principles:

Proximity Compatibility

I grouped related module readings (like fluid levels, nozzle health, and pressure) into hierarchical cards. This minimizes the need for the technician's eyes to scan wildly across the tablet.

Mental Models & Pictorial Realism

I included a visual 3D diagram of the UAV on the dashboard. This “real-world mapping” allows the technician to instantly connect a digital alert to a physical part of the drone.

Salience Compatibility

I applied strict color-coding (green/yellow/red) to draw immediate attention to critical issues. For example, a blocked nozzle alert is highlighted in red at the top right of the screen to ensure instant visibility.

4. Integrating AI for Troubleshooting

Agricultural technicians aren't always drone software experts. To bridge this gap, I integrated an AI Troubleshooting Chatbot into the interface. If a module fails, the chatbot provides predictive aiding—guiding the user toward effective fixes step-by-step, or allowing them to escalate the issue to a specialist via a centralized messaging hub.

04 — Key Solutions / Final Design

A functional, easily digestible tablet workspace

The final product for the Technician is a highly functional, easily digestible tablet workspace:

Centralized Health Monitoring

Hierarchical status cards provide instant updates on fluid levels, pressure, and temperature.

Actionable Alerts

Pop-up alert windows provide not just the problem, but recommended next steps to resolve the issue, supporting top-down processing.

Maintenance Memory

Automated logs serve as “external memory,” tracking all past issues, resolutions, and timestamps for the UAV and its attachments so the technician doesn't have to rely on memory.

06 — Reflection

Designing for cognitive limits in the field

Designing the Technician interface for high-stress, in-field environments reinforced the importance of designing for human cognitive limits. By strictly applying human factors principles—like proximity compatibility and predictive aiding—I was able to justify every layout decision objectively.

The most rewarding part of this project was taking a highly complex, multi-attachment system and simplifying it into an interface that actually helps the user solve problems rather than just displaying errors.

07 — Summary / Impact

Problems solved, goals achieved, and project vision
