Overview

An AI-powered receipt splitter that makes group dining effortless

Dining out with friends is fun, but splitting the bill — especially in the U.S. with complex tax and tip calculations — often ends in awkward math, frustration, and someone overpaying.

Pic2Split is an intelligent, AI-powered web application that eliminates this friction. By utilizing PaddleOCR and Gemini-2.5, users can snap a photo of their receipt, assign items to friends, and instantly share the final split. Best of all, it's web-based — no app downloads required. I led the end-to-end UX research and product design, collaborating closely with engineering to bring this complex AI utility to a polished, user-friendly reality.

01 — Problem Statement

Existing tools created friction exactly where users needed speed and trust

Through user interviews, heuristic evaluations of competitors, and market research, we identified three critical gaps in existing bill-splitting solutions: tedious manual entry, error-prone tax and tip calculations, and high adoption friction for one-time group use.

Manual entry is tedious

Apps like Splitwise require users to type every item and price by hand, turning one dinner into 5–10 minutes of cleanup work.

Tax and tip are confusing

Proportional tax and tip calculations across multiple people are error-prone and stressful to verify.

Adoption is too high-friction

Requiring a group to download an app and create accounts for one meal creates immediate drop-off.

OCR Accuracy
 OCR results were often inaccurate or misclassified (avg. 2.2/5). Users needed to manually correct totals. Optimized OCR algorithm and added a receipt review step, allowing users to compare scanned text with the original image.
Layout & Interface
 Layout satisfaction was low (2.0/5). Users described the interface as cluttered and visually confusing. Redesigned UI layout for clarity — simplified structure, improved spacing, and clearer icons to create a smoother experience.
AI Trust & Transparency
 Users questioned AI fairness and wanted to understand how totals were calculated (trust score 2.6/5). Added side-by-side receipt review and transparent calculation display to increase user confidence in results.
Guidance After Scanning
 Users felt lost after OCR processing and didn't know the next step. Introduced progress indicators and confirmation screens that show where the user is in the process and what to do next.
Feedback & Completion State
 The app didn't confirm task completion; users were uncertain if actions succeeded. Added a completion page after group or split creation, giving clear confirmation and next-step options.
Workflow Flexibility
 Creating groups and splits were separate, linear steps that confused users. Users can now add a split immediately after creating a group or create a group after completing a bill, ensuring smoother task flow.
User Account & Data Saving
 No account system; users couldn't save or revisit past bills. Implemented account creation to save data, enabling future access to previous receipts and groups.
Overall User Experience
 Process felt disjointed and lacked visual polish. Users struggled to stay oriented. Delivered a more guided, transparent, and responsive experience focused on clarity, trust, and ease of use.
02 — Project Goals

Three goals guided the MVP scope and every design decision

The research findings shaped a focused set of design goals that defined what success looked like — not just for the final product, but for each sprint and screen decision along the way.

Goal 01

Zero friction

Build a web-first product so groups can use it immediately without downloads or forced account creation.

Goal 02

Speed

Reduce a 5–10 minute manual splitting task to seconds by using OCR as the starting point instead of typing.

Goal 03

Accuracy and trust

Design clear correction and error-handling flows so users stay confident when AI or OCR gets something wrong.

01
Research & Heuristic Evaluation — competitors hid the hardest part

Most apps buried shared-item handling, proportional tax/tip adjustments, and late-stage corrections — exactly where users needed the most confidence. I documented these blockers in a heuristic matrix, which became the foundation for Pic2Split's core design mandate: make the hard math visible.

02
Mapping the User Flow — a technical pipeline reframed as a guided sequence

The backend was complex: upload, AI processing, data extraction, item assignment, tax/tip math, and sharing. I designed an 18-screen core flow prioritising clear progress indicators so first-time users always knew where they were and what to do next.

03
Wireframing & Edge Cases — edge cases are part of the product, not an afterthought

Low-fidelity wireframes resolved three critical requirements: clear labels distinguishing "Group Name" from "Member Name," friendly empty states for new users, and automatic rounding logic that assigns 1–2 cent differences to the largest spender — all before any UI work began.

04 — Usability Testing

Testing revealed three critical gaps in AI trust, input clarity, and visual polish

In October 2025, I conducted usability testing with 6 participants using our initial prototype. Average completion time was 4:30–5:38 minutes. The feedback was eye-opening — speed alone was not enough. Users needed trust, clarity, and polish before they would rely on the output.

07 — Reflection

Designing for AI unpredictability was the core UX lesson

Designing Pic2Split was a masterclass in accepting that perfection is impossible when you rely on LLMs and OCR. A receipt will inevitably be crumpled, or a font will be weird. The strongest design decision was not hiding AI errors — it was giving users a frictionless, empowering way to correct them.

Key Takeaway

A great UX doesn't hide AI errors — it provides users with a frictionless, empowering way to manually correct them. Pic2Split's Correction Flow became the most trust-building feature in the entire product.

Collaborating closely with React and Spring Boot engineers also taught me how to balance ideal user flows against strict technical constraints and latency issues — for example, designing engaging loading states while the AI parses the image kept the experience feeling responsive even when the backend took several seconds.
